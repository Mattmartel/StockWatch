New Zealand Media and Entertainment — NZX: NZM

NZME is New Zealand's largest news publisher and audio broadcaster, operating the NZ Herald, BusinessDesk, Newstalk ZB, iHeartRadio and OneRoof. Revenue is split across digital and print advertising (~72%), subscriptions and circulation (~23%) and other services. The company has ~28,000 BusinessDesk subscribers and ~180,000 Herald Premium subscribers.

FY2025 was a cost-led recovery: Operating EBITDA rose 15% to $62.3m despite flat revenue ($345m, −1.3% YoY), driven by a first-half restructuring programme that reduced people costs 4% and print costs 9%. The closure of 14 community newspaper titles in late 2024 reduced revenue slightly on a reported basis but improved margin. Digital subscription revenue grew 3% to ~$78m, and digital audio revenue grew 10%. Net debt fell to $15.5m (0.25× EBITDA), well inside the target range of 0.5–1.0×, supporting a flat 9.0 cent dividend.

The key structural question is whether digital subscription and advertising growth can offset continued print decline. NZME trades at ~3.8× EV/EBITDA — cheap by global media standards. The single broker FY2026 consensus points to EBITDA of $66.5m (+6.7%) and NPAT of $23.7m (+81% on statutory FY2025), with flat dividends of 9.0 cents. The most recent dividend declared was 6.0 cents per share (payable 18 March 2026, record 6 March 2026).

Sky Network Television — NZX/ASX: SKT

Sky is New Zealand's dominant pay-TV and streaming operator, delivering sport and entertainment via satellite (Sky Box), streaming (Neon, Sky Sport Now), broadband and commercial services. Sky Box subscription remains the revenue anchor at ~62% of total revenue ($751m in FY25), but streaming, broadband and advertising are all growing. Sky acquired Discovery NZ (now Sky Free — TV3, ThreeNow) for $1 in August 2025, materially expanding its free-to-air and BVOD presence.

FY2025 (year ended 30 June 2025) was dominated by the accelerated migration to a new satellite (KoreaSat6, completed April 2025), which disrupted customer engagement, caused ~$29m of one-off costs and delayed planned revenue initiatives. Statutory NPAT of $20.6m significantly understates underlying performance — Adjusted NPAT was $41.1m and Adjusted EBITDA $148.5m (−3% YoY). Programming costs at 50.9% of revenue remain above the 47–49% target. Sky has no drawn bank debt and $32.4m cash, supporting a 15.8% dividend increase to 22.0 cents, with 30 cents guided for FY26.

The HY26 result (6 months to 31 Dec 2025) confirmed the FY26 recovery: underlying revenue +8% to $415m, EBITDA +29% to $78.2m, and closing cash of $100m. FY26 broker consensus forecasts NPAT of $69.2m and a 30 cent dividend (+36%). Sky trades at ~2.6× EV/Adj. EBITDA on trailing earnings — cheap relative to global pay-TV peers, with the Discovery NZ acquisition and NZR deal as key FY26 catalysts. $244m goodwill is the primary balance sheet risk.

NZME and Sky TV are the two largest independent media companies listed on the NZX, with a combined market capitalisation of ~$630m and combined revenue of ~$1.1 billion. Both are navigating the same macro environment — a prolonged New Zealand advertising and consumer spending slowdown — but face structurally different challenges: NZME is managing the transition from print to digital publishing and audio; Sky is managing the shift from satellite to streaming delivery.

Both companies are generating strong free cash flow relative to market cap, carrying conservative balance sheets (minimal bank debt) and paying above-market dividend yields. Sky's most recent half-year result (HY26, to Dec 2025) showed underlying EBITDA +29% and the FY26 interim dividend up 76.5% to 15 cents — 30 cents guided for the full year. NZME's single broker FY2026 consensus forecasts EBITDA of $66.5m (+6.7%) with dividends flat at 9 cents. On EV/EBITDA both trade at significant discounts to global media peers (typically 5–10×), reflecting NZ market size constraints and structural concerns specific to each business. Sky is cheaper on this metric (~2.6×) than NZME (~3.8×), but carries more balance sheet risk through $244m of goodwill and large programming cost commitments. NZME's risk is more gradual — continued print and advertising decline — with less downside concentration.

Note on comparability: NZME reports on a December year-end; Sky TV on a June year-end. The figures shown represent each company's most recently completed full financial year, covering different 12-month periods. Both use non-GAAP operating metrics that exclude non-recurring items — NZME's "Operating EBITDA" and Sky's "Adjusted EBITDA" are the primary management metrics for each.
